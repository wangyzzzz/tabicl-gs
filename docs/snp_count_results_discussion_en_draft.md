# SNP Count Ablation: Results + Discussion (English Draft)

## Suggested subsection title

Marker-density scaling reveals sustained gains from prior-integrated TabICL fusion

## Results paragraph

To assess whether the fusion framework remains effective as marker information becomes richer, we evaluated a SNP-count ablation across 8 representative traits from 4 datasets under three marker-density settings (`2K`, `10K`, and `50K`). The `10K` condition reused the main `5.4-duli-liudang` results, whereas the `2K` and `50K` conditions were rerun within the same decoupled pipeline, with marker-specific `best block` re-optimized before archiving `no_prior-TabICL`, `BayesB`, `GBLUP`, and `RKHS`, from which all single-prior and triple-prior fusion outputs were reconstructed. Across the 8 traits, the mean Pearson correlation of `triple-prior-TabICL` increased from `0.6201` at `2K` markers to `0.6435` at `10K` and `0.6675` at `50K`, indicating that the overall ceiling of the fusion framework continued to improve with increasing marker density. This gain was not simply explained by a uniform advantage over every baseline. Relative to `BayesB`, the average improvement of `triple-prior-TabICL` remained positive but became smaller as SNP density increased (`+2.92%`, `+2.34%`, and `+1.48%` at `2K`, `10K`, and `50K`, respectively), suggesting partial convergence as `BayesB` itself benefited from denser marker information. By contrast, the relative advantage of `triple-prior-TabICL` over `GBLUP` and `RKHS` remained consistently positive and became more pronounced at higher marker density, reaching `+6.47%` and `+5.64%`, respectively, at `50K` markers. A similar pattern was observed for single-prior fusion: `single_gblup_two_step_ls` showed the most stable improvement over its corresponding prior, increasing from `+2.14%` at `2K` to `+2.57%` at `50K`, whereas `single_rkhs_two_step_ls` displayed a smaller gain under sparse markers but a clearer advantage at `50K`; `single_bayesb_two_step_ls` remained beneficial across all three marker settings, although its average gain gradually contracted. At the trait level, most trajectories were directionally favorable but not universally monotonic: 6 of the 8 traits showed non-decreasing `triple-prior-TabICL` performance from `2K` to `10K` to `50K`, whereas `grain_weight` and `bbd` deviated from this pattern. Moreover, although `triple-prior-TabICL` outperformed the strongest baseline in all 8 traits at `10K`, two rice traits (`grain_weight` and `grain_width`) were overtaken by `BayesB` at `50K`, even though `triple-prior-TabICL` remained superior to `GBLUP` and `RKHS` in the same setting. These exceptions argue against a simplistic interpretation that more markers or more priors necessarily yield unconditional improvement for every trait.

## Discussion / concluding paragraph

Taken together, the SNP-count ablation indicates that the utility of prior-integrated TabICL is scalable with marker density, but in a trait-dependent rather than universal manner. The key signal is not that all traits improve monotonically as SNP number increases, but that the fusion framework continues to extract complementary predictive information as genomic resolution becomes richer. In particular, the sustained and even enlarged gains over `GBLUP` and `RKHS` at `50K` markers suggest that increasing marker density does not diminish the value of TabICL-based integration; instead, it may provide additional opportunity for data-driven representations and statistical priors to interact productively. At the same time, the weaker relative gain over `BayesB` at higher marker density, and the fact that a few traits remain best captured by a single strong baseline, emphasize an important constraint on interpretation: the benefit of multi-prior fusion depends not only on the number of priors available, but also on how well those priors match the genetic architecture of the target trait. Thus, the marker-count experiment strengthens the main conclusion of this study in the same way as the sample-size analysis: the value of TabICL does not lie in uniformly replacing statistical genomic models, but in serving as a flexible fusion component whose contribution becomes apparent when complementary prior structures are available and informative.
